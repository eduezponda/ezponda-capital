## The Setup

There is a specific type of opportunity in junior mining that only appears occasionally: a company with real, producing assets, a credible multi-year growth pipeline, and a market that has not yet priced in what the business will look like in 24 months. Mako Mining is that opportunity today.

This is not a speculative play on exploration upside or a turnaround of a broken balance sheet. It is a straightforward capital allocation case: buying productive, growing assets at a price that implies the market is either not paying attention or unwilling to wait. The thesis mirrors almost exactly the rationale behind buying Aris Mining at $6, a stock that now trades at $20. Same logic, different vehicle.

The three ingredients that make this work: meaningful free cash flow at current production, a credible multi-year growth path that nearly triples output by 2028, and long-life assets that give the portfolio durability beyond the obvious near-term catalysts.

---

## Why Current Multiples Are Misleading

At today's enterprise value of roughly $475M, the market is essentially pricing Mako on its 2026 production profile alone: approximately 60,000 oz between Nicaragua (San Albino) and Arizona (Moss Mine), generating around $158M in gross margin at a $4,500 gold price. That gives a margin-to-EV ratio of about 33%. Cheap, but not extraordinary on that number alone.

The misvaluation lies in what happens over the next two years. Mako is not a single-asset company selling its mine life to the market. It is an asset aggregator in the middle of a production build-out: two mines producing today, one under construction (Mt. Hamilton, Nevada), and one at final pre-construction stage (Eagle Mountain, Guyana). Investors pricing the company on 2026 production are paying no attention to the growth that has already been funded and planned.

## The Four-Asset Portfolio

**San Albino (Nicaragua)**

The core engine. Producing 40,000 oz per year at $1,800 AISC, this is a high-margin asset generating approximately $108M in margin annually at $4,500 gold. The mine has roughly 6.3 years of M&I resource life at current production rates, shorter than the rest of the portfolio, which is exactly why the growth assets matter. San Albino is not a long-life mine, but it generates the cash that finances everything else.

**Moss Mine (Arizona)**

The second producing asset. At 30,000 oz annual production and $2,000 AISC, it adds $75M in margin and, crucially, carries approximately 23 years of M&I resource life. This is the portfolio's anchor for longevity: a low-cost, long-duration mine that will outlast most of the growth assets coming online.

**Mt. Hamilton (Nevada)**

Projected to enter production in 2027, ramping to 55,000 oz per year in 2028 at $2,270 AISC. Capex is estimated at $65M, assumed fundable through operating cash flow. At $4,500 gold, this asset contributes roughly $123M in margin at full production. With approximately 10.5 years of M&I resource life, it is a mid-duration, high-margin addition to the portfolio.

**Eagle Mountain (Guyana)**

The portfolio's most exciting asset and its biggest wildcard. Targeting 66,000 oz per year in 2028 at an initial PEA AISC of $829/oz, one of the lowest cost profiles at any development-stage gold project globally. The Indicated resource stands at 1.18M oz, implying approximately 17.9 years of mine life. Capex is estimated at $100M. Even if real-world costs come in double the PEA figure, Eagle Mountain remains transformational for Mako's earnings profile.

## EV Compression: The Hidden Kicker

Most investors look at this as a static valuation. The more interesting angle is dynamic.

San Albino and Moss are generating free cash flow right now. That cash accumulates on the balance sheet. The capex for Mt. Hamilton ($65M) and Eagle Mountain ($100M) is large, but it is being financed out of operating cash rather than equity dilution. As FCF compounds and capex spend completes, the net enterprise value compresses, even if the stock price stays flat.

By year-end 2028, with a conservative after-tax FCF assumption of $330M at a blended AISC of $1,900/oz and $4,500 gold, the forward EV/FCF multiple falls to approximately **0.6x.** The company would be generating its entire enterprise value in free cash flow every seven and a half months.

This is not a heroic forecast. It is the mechanical consequence of bringing two already-funded mines online while accumulating cash from two existing producers.

## What Has to Go Right

Three assumptions carry real weight in this thesis.

**Gold at $4,500.** This is not a conservative number. The margin profile survives meaningfully at $3,500, but the 2028 FCF story and valuation multiple expansion compress. Investors should stress-test the thesis at $3,000 gold before sizing.

**Mt. Hamilton on schedule.** Construction is expected to start April 2026 with a 14-month timeline, targeting a 2027 ramp. The first critical milestone is real but sensitive to execution. A site visit in April 2025 found no activity on site and confirmed the mine was not yet in construction, which is consistent with management's own timeline, but underscores that labor mobilization and logistical readiness in rural Nevada are the key risks to monitor through 2026. This is the thesis's most fragile near-term assumption.

**Eagle Mountain AISC.** The $829/oz PEA figure will rise with cost inflation and real-world logistics in Guyana. Even at $1,600/oz it is a transformational asset. Even at $1,900/oz it is still the cheapest mine in the portfolio. The thesis does not require the PEA to hold: it requires Eagle Mountain to be profitable, which almost any reasonable scenario supports.

**Nicaragua political risk.** San Albino is the cash engine that finances everything. Nicaragua carries jurisdiction risk that cannot be fully modeled and likely explains part of the valuation discount. It is a real risk, not a theoretical one, and must be sized accordingly.

## Final Investment View

The logic is not complicated. Mako is a real business with real assets generating real cash flow. It is building two additional mines from that cash flow. When those mines come online in 2028, production nearly triples, free cash flow exceeds the current enterprise value on a forward basis, and the market will have no choice but to reprice it.

The analog is Aris Mining, bought at $6 when the thesis was equally straightforward, equally dependent on execution, and equally ignored. Aris now trades at $20.

Mako offers similar asymmetry. The downside is a company that executes partially (slower timelines, higher costs) and still generates strong FCF from two producing mines at current gold prices. The upside is a multi-bag return as all four assets reach steady state.

Own it. Wait.
